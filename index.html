If the framing of your security team is that engineers don’t need to worry about security, this is what inevitably happens #
So you wind up generating a lot of extra security work with your engineering activity, after erecting a wall between them.

If you take the queueing analogies too seriously you might expect your overall development throughput to slow down, since the security queue can’t keep up. But in practice this mostly doesn’t happen. The weak point in this setup is the collective will to do security work, so it just doesn’t get done. The security queue overflows and explodes.

How do we tear down parochialism and ego? #
Yes, growth hacking is a bad word and everything. But I have started growth teams twice and the one weird trick to making marketing start to work is to … try to help? It’s crazy.

I inherited a situation where SRE was doing all of the deploys for the company, truly partying like it was 1999. I pushed to switch that to developers shipping their own code. There was some hesitation, the worry being that developers would break stuff and not fix it. But the better angels of our nature prevailed, engineers largely resolved their own incidents right away. Product engineers actually asked to be handed the pagers! It was heartwarming.

In the industry zeitgeist, devops went through the “this is a thing we need, so it’s a role” arc and you eventually saw job listings for devops engineers. Then eventually it was a tool you were buying, and the tool would let you rub some devops on things. Then it went out of fashion.
But the entire idea was that the roles should exist with a blurry line of responsibility.
This was just two friends of mine from Flickr standing on stage saying: “hey I’m John, hey I’m Paul, we are friends and cooperate.”
Working at a high-functioning eng org. meant just taking this extremely seriously, initially between dev and ops.
